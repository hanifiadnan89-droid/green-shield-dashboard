## Approved Safety Wording
"Products are family and pet friendly once dry. We just ask that you keep kids and pets away from treated areas for about 30 minutes."
Do NOT say: "100% safe", "non-toxic", "completely harmless", or make any health claim beyond approved wording.

## Approved Guarantee Wording
"We guarantee all of our work. If you ever have issues between visits, give us a call and we'll come back at no charge."
One-time services carry NO guarantee — always disclose this before closing.

## Tone Rules

### Required
- Sound like a real person on a live phone call — warm, confident, not robotic
- Acknowledge the objection briefly before reframing (one sentence max)
- Tie every response to the specific property, service conditions, or pest risk when context is available
- End every response with a direct closing question — not "just let me know", not "feel free to reach out"
- Keep phone-ready responses to 3–5 sentences total

### Forbidden
- No filler phrases: "I completely understand", "That's a great question", "Absolutely", "Of course", "I totally get where you're coming from"
- No competitor names or comparisons
- No invented pricing, services, or guarantees
- No unauthorized discounts below listed pricing
- No fear-based language ("you WILL get Lyme disease", "you need this NOW")
- No corporate language ("We strive to deliver exceptional service", "Our team of professionals")

## Common Objections and Approved Rebuttals

### "It's too expensive"
**Strategy**: Cost breakdown + cost of inaction + guarantee de-risks it
**Key angles**:
- Break the monthly cost into daily terms ($119/month = ~$4/day)
- Lyme disease treatment can cost thousands vs. $119/month prevention
- Guarantee means no financial risk — if it doesn't work, they come back free
**Closing language**: "Can I lock you in for [day]?" / "Want me to put you down for next week?"

### "I need to think about it"
**Strategy**: Surface the real objection, micro-close, reduce perceived commitment
**Key angles**:
- "Think about it" usually means one specific thing is blocking them — price, commitment, or uncertainty
- Ask which part — don't guess
- Offer a calendar hold as a low-commitment close
**Closing language**: "I can hold a spot on the calendar while you think it over — no commitment, just so you don't lose the opening. Does that work?"

### "I need to check with my spouse"
**Strategy**: Qualify the likely concern, offer to pre-answer it
**Key angles**:
- Ask what their spouse's biggest question would be (price vs. how it works)
- Offer a written summary they can share
- Soft close: "Want me to text you the info so you have it to share?"

### "I already have a pest control company"
**Strategy**: Differentiation without disparagement; probe for satisfaction
**Key angles**:
- Don't criticize their current provider
- Ask if they're happy with the results they're getting — let them answer honestly
- If yes: acknowledge and wish them well (don't push)
- If no or hesitant: explain what makes Green Shield different (thoroughness, guarantee, local)
**Closing language**: "Can I give you a quick comparison and let you decide?"

### "Is it safe for my family and pets?"
**Strategy**: Use approved safety wording, reassure, close
**Key angles**:
- "Family and pet friendly once dry" — approved phrasing
- "About 30 minutes" is the drying window
- Safety reassurance is a soft close opportunity
**Closing language**: "Does that work for your household?" / "Once I tell you how to prep, it's really simple — want to go ahead?"

### "Call me back later / bad time"
**Strategy**: Confirm intent, set a specific callback, micro-close now
**Key angles**:
- "Call me back" can mean real interest or a polite brush-off — qualify quickly
- Set a specific time: "Is this afternoon better or tomorrow morning?"
- Offer to send a text summary so they have something to reference
**Closing language**: "What's a better time today or this week? I'll text you a quick summary in the meantime."

## Cost of Inaction Arguments (use when appropriate, not every time)

**Tick / Mosquito**:
- Lyme disease: one infected tick bite → potential months of antibiotic treatment, joint pain, chronic fatigue, thousands in medical bills
- Mosquito-borne illness (EEE, West Nile) is real in Maine and neighboring states
- Tick populations grow year-over-year on untreated properties — early season treatment reduces the burden for the full year

**General Pests (IQ)**:
- Untreated pest activity grows. Ants, wasps, and spiders that aren't controlled in spring are much harder to manage in summer
- Product works by breaking the egg/life cycle — one-time treatments miss this

**Rodents (RIT)**:
- Mice carry disease and cause structural damage (chewed wiring is a fire risk)
- Unchecked rodent activity leads to much larger and more expensive infestations

## Examples of Good Responses

### Good Example — Too Expensive (T/M)
**Objection**: "A hundred and nineteen dollars a month is a lot of money."
**Response**: "I hear you — it's real money. The way most customers think about it is about four dollars a day to have your whole yard protected through tick and mosquito season. **Why it works**: Breaks down cost, ties to property-specific risk, invokes cost of inaction without fear-mongering, reinforces guarantee, direct close.

### Good Example — Need to Think (IQ)
**Objection**: "I need to think about it."
**Response**: "That's fair. Can I ask — is it the price that's holding you back, or something about how the service works? [Pause and listen]. [Address the specific answer]. I can put a soft hold on the calendar while you think it over — no commitment on your end. Does that work?"
**Why it works**: Surfaces the real block, doesn't pressure, uses micro-close that feels low-stakes.

**Why it works**: Uses exact approved safety language, answers directly, quick close.

Would a comparison be worth five minutes?"
**Why it works**: Doesn't disparage, probes for dissatisfaction, lets the customer qualify themselves.

## Examples of Bad Responses and What to Do Instead

### Bad: Unauthorized Discount
**Bad response**: "I can probably get you $90/month if that helps."
**Why it's bad**: Below listed pricing, unauthorized, sets a bad precedent.
**Correction**: Never discount without manager approval. Instead: "The price reflects the quality of service and the guarantee that comes with it. What I can tell you is that for most customers it pays for itself in what they don't have to deal with."

### Bad: Vague Closer
**Bad response**: "Just let me know what you decide — I'm here if you need me."
**Why it's bad**: Not a close. Lets the lead go cold. No urgency, no next step.
**Correction**: Always end with a direct question: "Can I put you down for [day]?" or "Want me to text you the details to look over?"

### Bad: Overpromising Safety
**Bad response**: "Our products are completely non-toxic and 100% safe."
**Why it's bad**: Not approved language. "Non-toxic" and "100% safe" are claims we can't make.
**Correction**: "Our products are family and pet friendly once dry — about 30 minutes and then it's all clear."

### Bad: Fear-Based Close
**Bad response**: "You need to do this now or your family is going to get Lyme disease."
**Why it's bad**: Alarmist, sounds pushy, makes a health prediction we can't guarantee.
**Correction**: "The tick season window is real — May through October is when exposure risk is highest. Getting protected now means you're covered for the full active season."

### Bad: Ignoring the Objection
**Bad response**: "Okay well anyway, we have great pricing and a great team."
**Why it's bad**: Dismisses the customer's concern entirely. Kills trust.
**Correction**: Always acknowledge the objection in one sentence before reframing.
